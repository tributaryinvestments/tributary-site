This META options trade showcases an advanced ratio spread strategy executed with precision, timing, and risk management. Rather than a simple directional bet, this trade collected an initial credit of $1,290 while maintaining bullish exposure — and ultimately captured nearly $13,000 in total profit as META rallied from the low $600s toward $750. The trade was based on a previous earnings gap (window) that was "left open." The basis of the trade was that the high end of the gap at $750 would be natural resistance for the stock.

Trade Structure

The Ratio Call Spread

This was a 1:2 ratio call spread — a sophisticated strategy that combines income generation with directional exposure.

Initial Position (November 17, 2025)

  • Bought: 2 contracts at $650 strike @ $35.25 = $7,050 debit
  • Sold: 4 contracts at $700 strike @ $20.85 = $8,340 credit
  • Net Credit: $1,290

All contracts expired February 20, 2026, giving the trade approximately three months to work.

A ratio spread involves buying a certain number of options while selling more options at a higher strike. In this case, the 1:2 ratio created several strategic advantages:

  • Immediate Income: The position opened for a credit — the trade started profitable
  • Bullish Exposure: The long $650 calls provided delta and upside participation
  • Capped Risk Below: Below $650, the position profits from time decay and the initial credit
  • Managed Risk Above: The short $700 calls created naked exposure above that level, requiring active management

Position Management

Navigating the Rally

When META had a gap-down sell-off, the trader took the short calls off for less than 25% of the initial credit they provided. In doing so, all upside risk was removed, turning the $1,290 credit into a debit of only $664 — while still holding two long calls into earnings the following week.

Phase 1: Closing the Short Calls (January 21, 2026)

As META gapped down toward the $600–$610 level, the opportunity arose to remove all upside roadblocks ahead of earnings.

  • Bought back 2 contracts @ $4.87
  • Bought back 2 contracts @ $4.90

By closing at $4.87 and $4.90 (when they were sold for $20.85), approximately $16 per contract profit was locked in on the short side — about $6,400 total.

Phase 2: Riding the Long Calls (January 28, 2026)

With the short calls closed, the position was now simply long two $650 calls with no upside risk. As META continued its surge toward $750:

  • Sold 1 contract @ $44.26 — morning of earnings, before the print
  • Sold 1 contract @ $89.00 — the day after earnings

The Risk Management Masterclass

This trade's success came from adapting when the original thesis evolved:

Original Thesis: META rallies moderately, stays near or slightly above $700, ratio spread collects premium and modest appreciation.

Actual Scenario: META is going to rally to $700, and the last-minute gap down before earnings is an opportunity to improve trade profit potential.

Adaptation: Close shorts to eliminate unlimited risk, let long calls run to capture the extended move.

Key Takeaways

1
Ratio Spreads Generate Income While Maintaining Directional ExposureThis structure collected $1,290 upfront while keeping bullish participation — best of both worlds when managed properly.
2
Know Your Exit Plan for Each ScenarioThe trader clearly had a plan for what to do if META surged through $700. This wasn't luck — it was preparation meeting opportunity.
3
Risk Management Trumps Maximum ProfitBuying back the short calls and converting to a small net debit might feel uncomfortable, but it eliminates risk and preserves the account.
4
Ratio Spreads Require Active ManagementUnlike defined-risk spreads, ratio spreads have unlimited risk. This isn't a "set and forget" strategy — it demands monitoring and willingness to adjust.
5
Convert Positions When Thesis ChangesBy closing the short calls, the trader converted a complex ratio spread into a simple long call position — adapting to market conditions rather than stubbornly sticking to the original structure.
6
Credit Collection Provides CushionStarting with a credit meant the trade had a buffer. Even if META had stayed flat or declined slightly, the trade would have been profitable from time decay alone.
7
Separate Decisions by LegThe short calls were closed January 21st, the long calls held until January 28th — independent thinking about each component rather than managing everything as one unit.

Conclusion

Starting with a $1,290 credit and ending with $12,661 in total profit, this ratio spread achieved a remarkable 980% return by combining income generation, directional exposure, and above all, expert risk management.

For traders studying ratio spreads, this recap illustrates both the profit potential and the critical importance of active management. These strategies can generate impressive returns, but they demand vigilance, flexibility, and the willingness to deviate from the original plan when market conditions require it.
